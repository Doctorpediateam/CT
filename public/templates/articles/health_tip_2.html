To LASIK or Not To LASIK: Basic Factors in LASIK Candidacy

You have been wearing glasses since the age of six. In your teens, you got contact lenses. Now with modern technology and advanced surgical procedures, the standards of aging and vision enhancement are pushed to a new horizon with the most appropriately futuristic technology: lasers. There are several requirements to be a good candidate for LASIK. Here are a few of the factors to consider before getting approval for LASIK.

The eye is a remarkable assembly of cells. These cells and our eyes can continuously change over time. People under the age of 18 routinely experience vision changes which may result in new prescriptions. Some young people have early cataracts and macular degeneration. While a young age is not a disqualifier for cataract Lasik surgery, it is a collaborative decision between you and your doctor. Lines of communication must remain open;  be sure to ask questions.

The pupil regulates the entrance of light into our eyes. It is the dark, black, circular opening at the eye’s center. The size of the pupil is a function of the amount of light in the room or environment. The pupils dilate in dark places; this allows more light to hit the retina so we can see in the dark. In brighter areas, the pupils constrict, limiting the light to the retina.

Large pupils in a person may be a factor in determining Lasik candidacy. Side effects of Lasik includes post-surgery vision detractors of optical glares, starbursts, and bright visual halos. Overall ocular health takes into consideration each patient’s own risk versus benefits analysis as to what they are willing to undergo.

Another factor in being considered a surgical candidate is the structure of your cornea. The cornea is the clear, transparent surface of the eye. It varies in thickness ranging from thick to thin. The size of the cornea is about 12 millimeters horizontally and 11 millimeters vertically. The average thickness is about 0.5 of a millimeter. Even the thinnest cornea has five layers. Just like a fingerprint, each person’s corneal structure is different. There is a higher risk of problems in people with thin corneas or corneal scarring. Discuss your corneal health with your healthcare provider.

Overall good health is another important factor, and it is always helpful to do a well-being self-assessment. People with uncontrolled diabetes, high levels of glucose in the blood, are not a wise candidate until their diabetes is well managed. Diabetes induces retinopathy, which is a condition that distends and broadens the pupils. The bigger pupil size may disqualify you from surgery. Cardiovascular disease, high blood pressure, blood flow, plaque formation, and blood coagulation are discussion points with your physician. A conversation regarding complications and your candidacy for the procedure is a significant part of the process.  Just the dialogue will alleviate your worries.

If you are in good physical and ocular health, Cataract Lasik is a highly valuable vision option. If you are unclear about any facet of this surgery, continue your research. Reach out to several providers and schedule a consultation appointment. Get a second and third opinion. It pays off in safety, peace of mind, and quality self-care.
